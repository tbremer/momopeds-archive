the chunky breakup is from the different oil. the detergents are supposed to break up the larger bits of materials. in a clutch tuned to last only 600-800 miles you will only notice a breakup near the end of its life. it can cause the pads to have a massive failure /if/ there is the possibility for it already. it gets into the small places and slowly slowly breaks it down. you'll only notice problems on long term, so if youre planning on burning through the clutch faster than a "stock" clutch, youre going to be okay. it only really increases your risk of the pads falling apart by a small bit. depending on the oil of course.

the stock puch was run on detergent-rich gear oil for about 5 years. (2001-2006) the pads fell apart and i had to replace the clutch. been running type F in that bike since.

After having my 3 shoe dialed in for great city riding, I played with oils.

did some digging on MA, found a lot of people running 30w (tried it, felt a lot like ATF), dug around more went to O'reily's found 5w30 (like it a lot more), went to Chicago with Beth, Amanda Bandit recommended 0w20, got some (I think Beth bought the first bottle?) REALLY liked it for the City, didn't care for it as much on the track.

Posted on PuchForums asking them what they use (knowing that it is more drag oriented), they recommended 40w.

Final summation you're shoes have to hit equally, or you'll have uneven pad wear, your clutch needs to slip into or close into your powerband, ATF or motor oil will degrade and needs to be changed frequently, & ATF at high temps slips less, even the nice stuff.

"Viscosity index (VI) is an arbitrary measure for the change of kinematic viscosity with temperature. It is used to characterise lubricating  oil in the automotive industry.

The viscosity of liquids decreases as temperature increases. The viscosity of a lubricant is closely related to its ability to reduce friction. Generally, the least viscous lubricant which still forces the two moving surfaces apart is desired. If the lubricant is too viscous, it will require a large amount of energy to move (as in honey); if it is too thin, the surfaces will rub and friction will increase."

"The VI scale was set up by the Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE). The temperatures chosen arbitrarily for reference are 100 and 210 ?F (37.8 and 98.9 ?C). The original scale only stretched between VI=0 (worst oil, naphthenic) and VI=100 (best oil, paraffinic) but since the conception of the scale better oils have also been produced, leading to VIs greater than 100 (see below).

VI improving additives and higher quality base oils are widely used nowadays which increase the VIs attainable beyond the value of 100. The Viscosity Index of synthetic oils ranges from 80 to over 400.vi=l-u/l-h*100.use this formula be find the viscosity index."

(from: //en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity_index)

So: 0 = no slip. 100 = all slip (for traditional oils) Sythetics have a higher range. 80 = low slip // 400+ = high slip





mobil 1 full syn at running temp has a lower viscosity over ##w20.
