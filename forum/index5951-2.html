Post by: Mike Pee on April 04, 2009, 11:31:56 pm
Okay, ghetto za50 clutch cable fix time.  This has gotten me home multiple times.
Scenario A: Cutch cable breaks at the handlebars, but is still attached in the engine:
- It will be too short to use the cable in the normal clutch engagement hand lever.
- Turn your handlebars all the way to the right
- Wrap the broken cable tight around the right fork tube
- Hold the end with your hand, preferably gloved
- Turn the handlebar to your left while on the stand, and your clutch should engage
- Bam, your entire front end is now your clutch engagement system

Scenario B: Clutch cable breaks at the engine side
- It will be too short to use the cable in the normal clutch engagement hand lever.
- You need to unscrew the smallest brass-colored nut with the hole in it from the engine side, but don't take the larger silver-colored nut out
- You need to tie knot in the end of the cable through that nut hole
- Screw that nut back in where it came from
- Turn your handlebars all the way to the right
- Wrap the broken cable tight around the right fork tube
- Hold the end with your hand, preferably gloved
- Turn the handlebar to your left while on the stand, and your clutch should engage
- Bam, your entire front end is now your clutch engagement system

Now, those are emergency fixes to get you home, and a few rides beyond.  Ideally you get a new clutch cable.  All they are are open-ended throttle cables.  Put the side with the nub on it through that holey nut, screw that in, put the open end through the clutch engagement handle and you're back to stock.  Don't ever take that bigger silver long nut spacer thing out.  It's a bitch to get back in.  Just take the brass colored one out of the top of the silver colored one.  You'll see it when you look.

The oil filler screw is unneeded.  Those are old, brittle, and broke on both of my za50s.  All you need to do is fill the crankcase with oil through the small screw on the clutch side cover (side that says PUCH) until it dribbles out.  I covered the top hole with gorilla tape to keep shit from getting in.

ZA50s are a bitch, but you learn to live with them.

That little rod is attached to the choke plate inside the carb. When you push it down, it pushes the choke plate into the path of air movement. If you look down the hole in the back of your carb (where your air filter would usually be) you should be able to see the plate move up and down. the woodruff key is a little D shaped piece of metal that goes into a slot on the crankshaft, and into a slot in the flywheel. See, on inside of the flywheel there's a lobe, the lobe opens the points,,, so the flywheel and the crankshaft need to be aligned so that happens at approximately TDC.   The Stator, the plate that the points condensor,and induction coils are mounted to , is itself mounted on slotted holes , so that it can be rotated at little , one way and the other,,,that's your timing adjustment.  retard and advance.
   If you have a strong spark, and its sparking at close to TDC, even without a carb, just a little spritz of gas should lite it up.
  compression can be an issue, but to start with, if you hold your finger over the spar plug hole, and feel anything,, you probably have enough compression to get it to run..
   so take a little stick, and stick in the spark plug hole, and rotate the motor till thats at the very top...take a felt tip, and make a mark from the flywheel onto the motor...then with a flshlignt, look thru the slots, turn the motor in the direction ti runs,,,and watch for those points to just start to open,,,,you should be within a 1/4 inch on the flywheel.....post your results.

Someone here has gotta know about the woodruff key though.

One time someone told me to find TDC this way by using a piston stop: Rotate the flywheel until the piston hits the piston stop. Make a mark on the flywheel. Rotate the flywheel the other way until the piston hits the piston stop again. Make another mark on the flywheel (both marks referenced to the same point on the case ... I think). Then measure between those two marks -- the halfway point should be TDC. If you don't have a piston stop, you can make one with an old spark plug and some nuts and bolts (like <a href="//www.mopedarmy.com/forums/discuss/read.php?f=7&i=75752&t=75554">this</a> -- it's a timing tool, but if you get the right hardware on it, it works as a piston stop too). It is probably better to try and avoid removing the flywheel unless you have to take it off, but if you ever do want to remove it, you can just stuff some rope into the spark plug hole. That will block off part of your cylinder and stop the piston from moving, which will also keep your flywheel from turning so you can get the nut off.
