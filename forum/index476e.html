You're not after speed? So you're totally okay doing 25 or 30mph, so long as it gets up hills well? Keep in mind, these things are designed to do anywhere from 20-30mph stock. If you're perfectly happy doing 35mph or less, then keep the stock cylinder. I'd recommend either a Circuit or Estoril pipe, a high flow air filter, and a 3-shoe clutch (which you will have to tap and tune, but I'm guessing you can figure that out). Depending on what carb you have now, upgrading to the 15mm may also help a little, but that's a fairly expensive part for what is probably not a huge increase in speed/power. I would leave the stock gearing for now and see how you like it. If you get everything else on there and decide you wouldn't mind sacrificing a little more of your top end for some low end power, then gear it down. With the stock cylinder and a 15mm or smaller carb, I think you'll keep your reliability and stay above 75mpg.

If you get everything on there and you still aren't happy with the performance, it would be pretty easy to throw a 70cc kit on it (which ought to get you both more top speed and more power getting to that top speed), but there's no sense going into that now since I'm kind of doubting you'll end up kitting it.

There won't be a whole lot you can do to a Puch to get it to really climb hills (unless you have a running start). That is why they have pedals.  I would seriously consider getting a smaller rear free-wheel for your pedal chain.  That will help you out a ton with making your pedals actually useful.

When there is a rally and everyone is stuck at a stoplight right before a big hill all the non-variated bikes whether they are stock, kitted, clutch modified, etc. will all dog it up the hill.  A variated (constantly variable transmission) bike will pull right up it like nobodies business though.

I do not advise a smaller front drive sprocket.  It will be adverse to both reliability and fuel economy.

The best thing you could do it put a pipe on it designed for low-end. Match the exhaust port to the pipe.  Do not change the exhaust port shape or size on the inside of the cylinder wall until you know a lot more about two-stroke tuning.
