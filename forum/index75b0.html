Man, Rowe hit the nail on the head . . . we have, as a society, have put down the laborer, craftsman, mechanic, etc. for so many years as not worthy of respect.  It's no wonder young people won't go into the skilled trades, as they have been raised to believe that a welder is someone who is a failure because he didn't get a degree (even though he makes better money than at least 50% of those with college degrees!)

Way back when, nobody ever told Auguste Escoffier that he was a loser who decided all he could do was cook food.  Henry Ford was a mechanic, and thus couldn't do anything right?  Somebody has to fix plumbing, build houses, pave roads, drill for oil, weld those power plants, and so forth. Why does our society think we don't need those guys?

I have been seeing this coming for years, ever since I graduated high school.  The "vocational" people were the "failures" in school.  Now they are the ones with solid jobs and zero college debt.  There is certainly something to be said for a person that can create with their hands.  My father is business educated but really a blue collar man when it boils down to it.  My grandfather was a jack of all trades so that he could keep his farm running without failure.  My other grandfather did the same.  Both were woodworkers as a hobby.   I have felt like a blue collar man with a white collar education for years now, and I wish I had my grandfathers to influence me when I was growing up.   I have vowed to teach my children how to work with their hands AND their minds so that they can find their calling and still be able to make something if they need it. 

The closest thing I have to soothing my blue collar wants has been building mopeds, and without that I would be going crazy with idle hands.   I can tell you that packaging orders and doing accounting for my business is pretty boring work compared to lacing up a wheel.  I just feel so accomplished after I build something.
