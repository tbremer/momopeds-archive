You guys ever hear of the Arts and Craft Movement? 

Guys like John Ruskin, Elbert Hubbard, Charles and Henry Green, Rene Makintosh, Charles Limbert, William Morris, Gustav Stickley?  They saw the same problems a 100 years ago.  I'm a firm believer in the cyclic nature of history.  These are old problems that we've seen before.  History tells us that widespread problems tend to lead to reactions and broad movements. 

Those guys (and some ladies too) recognized that Americans had lost respect and appreciation for things that were made by people.  They feared for a loss of essential skills to build and repair things.  They saw handicraft and manual labor as integral for democracy, and did everything they could to change the way Americans saw the world.  They pushed for spaces sized for humans, art made for all to enjoy, humans living in tune with the landscape, and respect for manual labor and handicraft. 

The movement swept America and led to a new forms of art and architecture.  It dominated art and architecture for 30+ years,   The movement was on the decline when the great depression finally killed it.  and then you get the baby boom... assembly line houses... suburbs... sprawl... and again a lack of appreciation for all things "human"

Look around you.  Your neighbors throw out a complete patio set because one chair is damaged.  They rip out the interior of old homes and throw them away.  They buy a new car every year.  Build a multi-car garage that takes up the entire back yard. 

It's the throw away culture.  When you throw everything away, there's no need to appreciate the skill and time and resources it took to make it. 

A degree is not a waste of time or money, but I agree that too many folks are let into college and too many are given degrees for too little work.  It cheapens the efforts of folks who do deserve degrees and it cheapens the appreciation for folks who know how to build and repair.  Now that everyone has a BA in something, you've got to get an MA before the degree makes a difference.  Manual labor is valuable and can even be therapeutic for the mind, but as you age, you find that the body you depend on for money will eventually wear out.  When your body wears out - then what have you got?
