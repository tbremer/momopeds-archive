A few people say that you're supposed to read the base of the plug, and to see down that far, you have to actually cut it. Which is more correct?

Yeah, to get the most accurate reading you should chop the threaded portion off so you can see the base of the insulator. But for mopeds this is mostly a pain and unnecessary. The slick (and I'd probably say proper) thing to do is put your setup on a dyno, run it with a load and stick a wideband AFR probe in the exhaust pipe to measure fuel mix. But we don't really have either of those things here. We did this to our karts (mostly 4t, I was the 2t weirdo), and even then we had to go to the dyno facility an hour away. THEY all ran alcohol also, so it was a little harder for them to get tuned just right, thus the need for the WB.

Haha, welllllll I try to be a little more proactive. With the MK80 it was like 10-15 hours. With the Hobbit it was almost never. Unless you really know what you're doing, the idea is not so much to use it as a tuning TOOL and use it as a tuning AID. The only reason it's halfway viable as a tuning tool for us is because we're air cooled and pulling the head on an AC bike takes no time at all. On a LC motor it's feasible to pull the head to just look at the burn pattern, and I think we should treat our bikes the same as well. Just tune it right from the plug, time it right, and every few hundred miles, pull things apart at least a little bit and look at cues from your motor. Things like burn pattern, cylinder scarring (to sand high spots on the piston), carbon buildup, bearing play, etc..

Oop, Slink posted before I could respond to Mike. I think most of the plug reading charts tell you how a plug looks in regards to timing. There's no real reading for retarded timing (you'll just get backfires, sloppy throttle response, poor performance, etc, you know this), but if your timing is too advanced you'll get white deposits on the VERY tip of the plug, either on the center electrode or ground strap (or both), and in EXTREME cases, the spark plug will begin to melt or erode due to excessive timing; but our motors tend to burn holes in pistons before that happens.

SO, for example, if you have a black plug with a white tip, you should probably look at either your timing advance or point gap (too wide of a point gap will lead to an erratic spark, which could cause problems the same as advanced timing). Conversely, if you've got a brown plug with electrodes the same color as the insulator, you could probably advance your timing a little (with respect to head temperature), as the electrodes should ideally be clean.
