Quote
a lot of the casualties were at the Home Depot

This one definitely made me think. I bet the Home Depot here has no better protection -- either plan-wise or shelter-wise -- than the one that got hit did. I told my boss that we need an actual severe weather plan that is posted for employees. There is no good shelter in my store, although one of the adjacent stores has a basement. If they're closed when we need the basement though, that doesn't do us a lot of good. It would also be nice to have a set policy in place, like, if the sirens go off and the storm is actually going to hit us, we go to the shelter with customers and lock the store behind us. It's a lot easier to say, "Policy says we go next door right now, so that's what we're doing" rather than wasting time second-guessing ourselves.

How many of you saw the "first person" tornado video? There's no electricity, it's obviously storming REALLY badly outside ... knowing what happens, you kinda gotta think they knew that something really bad was about to happen. But then again, anyone growing up in the midwest has seen storms that knock out power and look really awful, and none of THOSE storms wiped out your city, right? That's potentially dangerous anecdotal evidence, as the video shows. Some people were asking if the sirens had gone off already, and nobody seemed to have a good idea of how significant the tornado threat was until the thing was right on top of them, at which point they substituted "staying away from the windows" for "running to the cooler." I don't want to sound like I'm dismissing their preparedness or cool heads, because I know that given the same situation, I could have done no better. The point is that, given the same situation, that's not the position I want to be in. I don't want to be standing there going, "Is this for real? What do I do?" I want to be sheltered somewhere safe, because company policy told me to get somewhere safe 15 minutes ago. This is how it worked at school, and in my dorms, and that's the way it should be in the business world as well.

They say the folks ran into the home depot for safety and huddled against the wall for safety.  The same walls that fell inward.  Chilling stuff.  Close to 1,500 folks unaccounted for... but a lot of that may be due to lost communications.  The Tornado itself was recently described as a multiple vortex tornado... sounds like it had several smaller tornadoes inside a larger vortex.  Must have been like a spiked ream going across the land.  Still can't believe that most of those house had no basements and no storm shelters. 

Back when I was in Grad School, I went to a lecture from the Kent State Chair of Geography.  He's a tornado scientist/meteorologist.  He explained how houses with attached garages are destroyed as the wind pressure gets into the garage, from there into the roofspace and then the house explodes from the inside.  He said that's why you often find the interior walls intact with the rest of the house destroyed.  His big area of research was evaluating the safety of being in an automobile.  Wind-tunnel experiments showed that compared to ditches and trailer houses, you're better off inside your car.  But nothing compares to a real basement or storm shelter. 


I agree with Linda about the sirens.  I watch Wunderground closely when bad weather comes in.  You can track the storms and watch for vortex signatures.  I was monitoring when the Lambert storm hit, but they kept setting off the sirens down here in South City - when nothing was around.  Frustrating.
