Cold/winter means that the air is more dense, which means you need a bigger jet to compensate for more oxygen getting into the combustion mix.  If you don't put in a bigger jet, you will run lean.

Warm/summer means the air is less dense, so you need a smaller jet to compensate.  If you don't put a smaller jet in you'll be running too rich.

This is why my maxi runs better on the way to work in the morning (when it's cooler outside) than it does when I head home at 4:30 when it's all warm out.  It's also why our bikes seem to run shittier in summer.

Gentlemen, I wish to jump into the Jetting discussion regarding weather changes.  I don't believe we should consider changing jets with the change in seasons.  The general barometric pressure of our location on earth does not significantly change with temperature changes.  Yes an engine runs better in cool weather (once you get it running) because the air is slightly more dense. 

However, while there is a minute change in engine performance with cooler air, only moving to a higher altitude should necessitate a change in jetting.  The density of the air is generally controlled by the number of air molecules above you pressing down on the air molecules at your altitude.  At higher altitudes, there are fewer molecules above you, so the air is generally less dense.  Automobile engines have, in the past, required a jetting change to give normal performance to people who permanently live in higher altitudes.  Modern fuel injected automobiles automatically adjust for altitude. 

Barometric pressure gives you an indication of the "general air density" I'm talking about.  The average barometric pressure in St. Louis is just under 30 inches of mercury.  Storms cause changes above and below this average.  As soon as you make a slight change in jetting for summer temperatures, a storm would come to town and your moped would run crappy again.
