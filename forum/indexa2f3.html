The octane number.. 87, 89, 93  refers to the number of carbon chains in the chemical structure of gas.  The larger the number the more carbon chains.  The more carbon chains, the hotter, more powerful, and more efficent the explosion will be once the gas is ignited.   Thats why race cars that run normal unleaded fuel run higher octane gas; however, they also have higher compression engines that can take advantage of the increases in explosive force. 

Your moped isn't really going to see much power gains from running 93 over 87.  Also I would be weary about getting gas from merinas.  Because their reservoir tanks are usually on or close to the water, they usually have a fair amount of water mixed in with the gas.

Woo, another octane thread with some misinformation. Wish I had a dollar for every time I came across one of these on the internets.

Octane has NOTHING to do with the amount of explosive force found in gasoline. This is a big misconception. In fact, higher octanes are more RESISTANT to ignition than lower octanes. If you've raised compression, advanced your ignition timing, widened your squish band; anything that increases cylinder pressure, you HAVE to run higher octane fuel, otherwise you will suffer engine damage. If you HAVEN'T changed anything in your motor, you WON'T see performance increases from running higher octane fuel. But, since mopeds use such an insignificant amount of fuel, I'd suggest that everyone buy higher octane, mostly because it's usually just a better fuel at the pump. Companies charge more, so it tends to be higher quality, with less octane additives and more fuel cleaner additives.

Brett: You're right about the carbon chains, but not about it being in different grades of gas. Different TYPES of fuel have different carbon chains. Diesel has more CCs than gas, Kerosene has more CCs than diesel. And yes, kerosene and diesel have more energy per unit volume than gasoline. Unfortunately, they're harder to burn. That's why diesel engines are generally big and heavy, and kerosene-burning jet engines are, well, jet engines. But again, different types of gasoline have the SAME explosive force per unit of volume, it's just that certain engines can artificially boost efficiency by compressing the mixture (high-comp or turbocharging), at the expense of mixture stability, thus the requirement for higher octane.

Also, Cal, you'll probably find out as I did, that most places in the country outside the Kansas City Midwest region sell 93 Octane. I was totally pumped to find that 93 was so widely available (there are only 4 stations in the KC metro area that sell 93, but it's everywhere in Saint Louis), because my car is turbocharged. I was able to remap my boost control system and gained about 2 PSI all around (with a pretty decent increase in power) because of the difference in octane.
