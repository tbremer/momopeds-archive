Re: WHAT DO YOU COLLECT BESIDES A MOPARD?
« Reply #49 on: April 27, 2011, 09:59:26 pm »
Don't use the heavy Edison Phone tone arm as they wore out the cylinders relatively fast.  Pick up a cheap magnetic cartridge (it MUST be a magnetic cartridge) somewhere and mount it on the end of a yardstick.  Insert about a 6" -8" piece of heavy wire (cut from a clothes hangar) through a hole in the yardstick about 10" from the end opposite the phono cartridge.  Wrap this wire about the middle with enough masking (or other type) tape to keep the yardstick held about the middle of the heavy wire when you fasten it in a vertical position to some sort of frame (an old picture frame? Or a frame made of scrap wood?).  This will provide the swivel point for up/down and left/right movement of the yardstick "tone arm".

Run your wires (thin as you can get . . . strip them from an old piece of telephone hookup wire) from the cartridge down to the swivel point (tape them about 3 places along the yardstick to keep them on the yardstick) and make a loose loop at the swivel point so the yardstick can easily move around without the wires pulling on it.  Fasten the wires to the support frame to keep them from moving around, then leave a few inches loose from where they are fastened to the frame -- the ends of these will be stripped to expose the wire ends which will be connected to an older stereo amp that has a PHONO input on the back.

The last thing is to put enough weight on the end of the yardstick opposite the cartridge to almost lift the cartridge "see saw" style . . . you want the weight of the cartridge on the cylinder to be only about 3 ounces which is about right to play the cylinder without wear on the grooves.

Have a stereo inclined friend who is also knowledgeable about electronics to hook those loose wire ends to a stereo cable with RCA connectors on the end to plug into the amp.  If the amp has a mono button, push it to the mono position (since the old cylinder is mono anyway) . . . adjust the Balance knob to get the sound balanced from both left and right speakers.

Then you run the cylinder using the Edison player and play up the music using your new ersatz electronic tone arm and stereo system.  If you have a CD recorder you can connect it to the "Tape Out" connection on the stereo amp and record the music on a CD (you could also record it on a CD with your computer if you have the connections for that).

Sorry if this was a bit long, but maybe it well help you to listen to, and record, the cylinder music with a minimum of wear to the cylinder.
