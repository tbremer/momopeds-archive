First job/worst job: Subway. There's a reason why, if you go to Taco Bell, or Wendy's, or McDonalds, or Burger King, or White Castle, or just about fast food establishment besides Subway, they have a person standing up front taking your order, and other people in the back making your food. It's because, if you were allowed to stand over the person making your food while it was being made, you'd be stupid, picky, rude, and you'd slow down what could otherwise be a much faster process. We pretty much can't get anything done without being interrupted by customers, which tends to make us resent customers. Of course, the customers themselves do a pretty good job making us hate them, just because nobody bothers to read or listen. Our menu design is awful and unintuitive, but we do get quite sick of being asked how much things cost; the menu is right behind me, and it's not SO terribly designed that you couldn't find an answer to your question if you spent 5 seconds reading. I also get sick of people not paying attention to their sandwiches -- like, "Oh hey, yeah, you're trying to find out what vegetables I want on my sandwich, but you don't mind yelling halfway across the store at me, because I'm super in a hurry and I NEED to fill up this drink cup RIGHT NOW." (Filling up a drink cup can be substituted with going to the bathroom, going out to your car, getting chips/apples, chasing your unruly child, sucking on your SO's face, joking around with your bros, gossiping with your sorority sisters, taking a phone call, texting ... ) Admittedly, I've worked at Subway for entirely too long, so I'm probably just bitter about it, but the whole thing really is a terrible design from the ground up.

Best (only other) job: GIS/office work at the Kansas Biological Survey. Everyone I worked with was just absurdly nice, I got to work on some interesting projects, and I learned a lot/got a lot of good experience. I suppose some of the stuff was kind of tedious, but most of it appealed to my inner anal perfectionist, so even that stuff wasn't too bad.

Dishwasher at Howard Johnson's in College Park, Maryland.  1963 & pay was 56 cents an hour.  Worked 8 hour evening shift . . . plus clean-up after the place closed was un-paid time.  That was standard restaurant practice back then - if the doors were locked you were off the payroll.  Also, minimum wage then was $1.25 an hour, but restaurants were exempt from the minimum wage rules.

Oh, also, you paid for anything you ate . . . less a generous 20% off the menu price for an employee discount!

Neat, huh?
