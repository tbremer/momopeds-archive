With NGK, the higher the number, the cooler the plug.

Honestly, on our bikes, the only thing that people need to do regarding plug heat ranges is run the absolute coldest plug they (as a mechanic) can tolerate. Like, I run B9HS, and haven't ever fouled one. If the plug got completely fouled more than maybe once every 20-30 hours, I'd switch down to a B8HS. But there's really NO advantage to running a hotter plug, except for less fouling. The disadvantage of running too hot of a plug is engine failure. I'd say the pros outweigh the cons.

EDIT: ^ That stuff was meant for people running kitted bikes. For a stock bike, I'd say run what the Mfr tells you to, minus one or two heat ranges. So, if the manual says to run a B6HS, for example, I'd run a B7HS or B8HS. When they made those manuals back in the 70s, they were using weird jungle juice for gas and dino cum for oil. Oils have gotten a LOT more advanced. Suggested oil mix ratios have gotten leaner and oil burns a LOT cleaner now, so there's no need to have a super hot plug to burn it all off.

There's another reason to keep your plug cooler. As temperature rises, resistance rises. This means more resistance for your spark, and more load on your electrical system/weaker spark. Not sure how much this affects running performance, but it's just something to ponder.
