Actually, it's because of the Bernoulli principle; that fast moving air has a much lower pressure than static air. This drop in pressure also causes a drop in temperature, which in turn causes the humidity to collect on the carburetor body (temperature below dew point). In extreme cases, the condensation can actually turn to ice; and in carbureted aircraft, has actually caused engine failure and crashes because the carburetor becomes completely iced over.

Woo, science!


But yeah, completely normal.

I'd hope you wouldn't have carb heat on when you're taking off.. You get a considerable manifold pressure drop, as well as the (in most powerplants) air source bypassing the main air filter. So you risk FOD and climbout incidents.. I'd never use heat on takeoff unless it was a VERY cold day, but then you'd have more pressing airframe/prop ice issues to worry about; but I ALWAYS use(d) heat when throttling back to descend.

I remember a Continental O-300 manual in a C172 that warned of carburetor ice from 20F-90F degrees.. Nuts.
