Today's stupid question (cylinder temps)
« on: February 05, 2010, 12:16:40 am »
I know most of you have seen (in person or pictures of) the soldered copper pipe intake on my Tomos. So far it seems like it's holding up, but I do wonder about the temperatures it is exposed to and how close those get to the temperature at which the solder melts. It seems like it would do very bad things to my engine if some of the solder happened to melt and either create an air leak or get sucked into the cylinder, ya know? I see temps of 400-ish or less as being "acceptable" for the cylinder head temp gauges, and I believe I've seen temps pushing 1000 degrees quoted for the exhaust/exhaust gasses (Justin, I think you wrote that). But what about the outside of the cylinder? And the intake port?

yeah, the higher flow of air should be cooling the intake down actually. i get condensation on the outside of mine after blasting hard. it depends on your solder, but most types have a melt point of between 280 to 580.

if you're worried about it, replace the solder joint closest to the intake with something else, jb weld or whatever. but if its not directly in contact with the engine mount, i wouldn't worry too much.

if you want to make it jankier chop up the cooling stack from a CPU cooler and attach that on it haha it'll dissipate the heat before it got to the solder. but would look really janked

those 400-550'ish C.H.T. readings are taken at the sparkplug, besides the exhaust it's probably the hotest external part of an engine.

you also have "evaporative cooling" going on in there, the intake is going to be pretty cool (comparatively).

I would be concerned with vibration damage first. the harmonics of an already poorly balanced engine, turning faster then it's designers intended can be pretty destructive.
