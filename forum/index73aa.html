You might start by attending some moped Fix-it days.  Ask what kind of problems attendees have had with their mopeds.  Learn how easy (or difficult or expensive) the fix was.  Then, as you shop you will know this or that problem with a moped you are viewing is an easy or difficult (or expensive) fix.  That way you can adjust how much you are willing to pay for said moped.  You would feel more comfortable knowing you are purchasing a moped where the only thing you see wrong is a dirty carburetor.

My personal experience???  If I were shopping for a moped, I would first decide if I'm looking for a ride that is or can be made to look like new, or if I'm looking for one that just works.  Not all of us can be a Jimmy and find a new 1977 moped for cheap, so the second choice would be best.  If the first choice is your goal, plan on taking a fair moped and making it look like new over time.

Next, you might look at the rust situation, both outside and especially inside the gas tank.  The outside rust that has pitted the chrome on a moped will never look great.  Rust in a gas tank will have you "jumping through hoops" to clean or treat the gas tank or will have you cleaning the carburetor on the side of Grand Avenue during rush hour.

Next, I would look at how common a moped is.  Finding parts for some brands is not easy.  For a first moped, try to pick a common brand (Puch) or a brand with a common engine (Puch-powered).  There are other brands where parts are available as well.  Even befoe you buy, go shopping for parts and write down the brands you would consider because lots of parts are out there.

Finally, I would suggest you not try to get every last ounce of performance out of your moped unless you can allow for a lot of "down time" or unless it's a second moped in your fleet.  From what I see, the more you soup up a moped, the more it breaks.

Good luck shopping,
Dave Gibbons

Ok, I guess for shopping purposes, I could say I paid $250 for my Vespa Grande at a pawn shop.  It was running, but barely.  I kitted it up to 64cc and changed the muffler to a free-flowing one.  I now have about $500 and a lot of hours in it.  It is extremely dependable, but then again, I did not go to extremes in getting performance out of it.  I can hit 30 easily and 35 over a l-o-n-g time.

I paid $80 for my non-running Puch-powered JC Penney at a county fair.  I invested another $200 and a lot of hours to get it running and to get some non-rusted rims and such.  It runs dependably and well for a completely stock ped.

When I say I have "a lot of hours" in my mopeds, I mean I spent time trying to reduce rust appearances and spent A LOT of time checking all timing, points, compression, lubrication, adjustments, etc. while learning how they work.  Anyone shopping for his first moped should not expect to just jump on it and ride unless he purchases from someone who did all that work for the buyer.  Even then, the buyer should spend lots of time learning how they work so he/she can fix it.

So yes, I have a couple of good mopeds.
