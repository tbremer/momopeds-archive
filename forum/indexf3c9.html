General Discussion / Re: General Moped Buying Advice?
« on: August 31, 2008, 07:53:13 am »
You might start by attending some moped Fix-it days.  Ask what kind of problems attendees have had with their mopeds.  Learn how easy (or difficult or expensive) the fix was.  Then, as you shop you will know this or that problem with a moped you are viewing is an easy or difficult (or expensive) fix.  That way you can adjust how much you are willing to pay for said moped.  You would feel more comfortable knowing you are purchasing a moped where the only thing you see wrong is a dirty carburetor.

My personal experience???  If I were shopping for a moped, I would first decide if I'm looking for a ride that is or can be made to look like new, or if I'm looking for one that just works.  Not all of us can be a Jimmy and find a new 1977 moped for cheap, so the second choice would be best.  If the first choice is your goal, plan on taking a fair moped and making it look like new over time.

Next, you might look at the rust situation, both outside and especially inside the gas tank.  The outside rust that has pitted the chrome on a moped will never look great.  Rust in a gas tank will have you "jumping through hoops" to clean or treat the gas tank or will have you cleaning the carburetor on the side of Grand Avenue during rush hour.

Next, I would look at how common a moped is.  Finding parts for some brands is not easy.  For a first moped, try to pick a common brand (Puch) or a brand with a common engine (Puch-powered).  There are other brands where parts are available as well.  Even befoe you buy, go shopping for parts and write down the brands you would consider because lots of parts are out there.

Finally, I would suggest you not try to get every last ounce of performance out of your moped unless you can allow for a lot of "down time" or unless it's a second moped in your fleet.  From what I see, the more you soup up a moped, the more it breaks.

Good luck shopping,
Dave Gibbons

Brian,

When you check plug color, look at the porcelian below the contacts.  Don't worry about the metal around the outer edge or the contacts themselves.  The porcelain gets hottest when the engine runs and that's when the color is developed. 

You might have heard of different "heat range" of plugs.  A hotter plug just has a longer porcelain.  It goes up higher into the metal around the outside of the plug (inside the threads) before it is bonded to the metal.  That means it cannot dump its heat as easily as one that contacts the outside metal just barely inside the threaded metal.  So it's the porcelain that develops the most heat and can burn to the proper color. 

I see your thoughts on the oil in the gasoline causing a darker tint to the porcelain color.  The article is talking about motorcycles.  Some do and some don't burn oil with the gasoline, but I think the article is talking about big bikes that don't burn oil with the gas so I assume they would have a lighter color.  The oil does cause oily deposits on the metal at the end of the threads for sure and could be darkening the porcelain as well.

Repair and Performance / Carburetor Tuning and plug chops
« on: July 05, 2008, 07:58:30 pm »
I found an article about motorcycle carburetor tuning and I would like us to put our heads together and see if we can apply the information to simpler moped carburetors.  I will first give you a link of where the article is, so you can read it yourself.  Then, I want to summarize key points that I'm hoping we can apply to moped carburetors that do not have all the adjustments a motorcycle carburetor has.  The article is at:
//www.johnsmotorcycleparts.com/Keihin_CV_tuning_tips.htm

What interests me from the article is the idea we should do multiple plug chops to see where our carburetor problem is.  AND we should always use NEW sparkplugs.  The article says motorcycle carburetors have 1.) idle circuits 2.) midrange circuits and 3.) high speed circuits.  I know most of our moped carburetors have the midrange and high circuits, but I have not figured out the idle circuits form mine yet.

The midrange circuit is the needle attached to the throttle slide.  The size, shape and position (up and down) of the needle gives us proper medium speed cruising.  The main jet controls fuel flow at wide open throttle (WOT).

The article tells us to do a plug chop by putting in a new plug, run the motorcycle at the range (right now, I'll say medium speed or WOT) and kill the engine and inspect the plug.  The article explains that we will no longer see the tan color we all see in the books about mopeds.  Leaded gasoline gave that tan color, and we don't have lead in the gasoline any more.  NOW WE NEED EGGSHELL WHITE!  We look at the ceramic surface below the spark gap and nothing else.  So, for example, we would put in a new plug, run the moped for a mile or two at mid-throttle, kill the engine and inspect the plug.  Then, we would find an open area, PUT IN A NEW PLUG and run the moped at WOT for a mile or two.  Again, we would kill the engine and do an inspection of the plug color.

Then, we need to figure out what our carburetor "idle circuit" is and do a third plug chop (with another new plug) with the engine idling.  Actually, the article implies this is the first plug chop, but you get the idea.

Interesting stuff!  Can we apply it to mopeds?

Repair and Performance / Re: Hi-comp head
« on: June 26, 2008, 09:27:49 am »
Have you changed to premium fuel?  I wonder what the end result compression ratio is.   When an engine exceeds a certain compression ratio, you need to upgrade the fuel to premium or you can have pre-ignition or "engine knock."  I always thought of 9.5:1 as requiring regular gas (what's that? 97 octane?) and 10.5:1 as needing premium, but I don't know the cutoff between those two.

Did the high-compression head say what the compression ratio would end up having?  I wonder how one would figure it. 

First, you can figure displacement mathematically with the cylinder dimensions of bore and stroke.  Or you could figure it by connecting a hose to the spark plug hole and run that hose to a graduated (measurement markings on the side) glass container held upside down under water.  Then, you would run the piston from bottom dead center to top dead center and measure the air pumped into the glass.  But that does not take into account the space above the piston in the head.

Second, I wonder how accurate a measurement you would get of just the head by removing it, holding it upside down and filling it with water.  Then dump out and measure the water.  You would then need to determine if the piston makes it all the way to the top of the cylinder.  If not, you must add the volume of the space from the top of the stroke to the bottom of the head. 

The true compression ratio would be the total volume from the first measurement plus the second compared to the second only.  You would call the second measurement "one" and see how many times the second measurement goes into the first.
